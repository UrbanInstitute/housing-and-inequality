Baseline income distribution We start by looking at the distribution of post-tax, post-transfer income without housing subsidies and deductions, dividing the population into twenty equal groups.
Now let’s look at what happens when we add in housing subsidies…

Adding in housing subsidies reduces inequality Housing subsidies totaled $36 billion in 2012. Incomes increased most for households with the lowest incomes.

Adding in the mortgage interest and real estate tax deductions slightly increases inequality. When the value of the mortgage interest deduction ($70 billion) and real estate tax deduction ($28.2 billion) are added back in on top of housing subsidies, incomes at the top of the distribution rise.

Housing subsidies offset the inequality brought about by housing deductions despite a third of the funding. Even though the total value of housing subsidies is slightly more than a third of the value of the mortgage interest and real estate tax deductions, housing subsidies accrue to low-income households and represent a larger share of those households’ incomes than the deductions represent for higher income households.
